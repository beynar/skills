# Information Architecture

Everything created at Shopify has an underlying foundation of information architecture. If you're a designer, content strategist, or UX developer, you're already doing IA work.

## Defining IA

Information architecture is the practice of organizing, structuring, and labeling content in an effective and sustainable way. Whether you're creating an app, part of the Shopify admin, a new feature, or an editorial page, these IA principles help you make content usable and findable.

## Why We Do IA

- **Simplify complexity:** Information should be structured and managed in the simplest way possible.
- **Support scalability:** Good IA practices help avoid overhauling previous work whenever the product grows or changes.
- **Create familiarity:** Regardless of how merchants access Shopify, it should feel like the same product. Creating a common experience extends beyond design to how we structure Shopify's content.

## Core IA Principles

### 1. Show Your Audience Where They Are

Successful wayfinding happens when your audience can make navigation decisions that fulfill their goal. For navigation to enable wayfinding:
- Establish multiple navigation schemes
- Use task-based navigation
- Integrate secondary navigational support (like breadcrumbs)

Navigation is a tool that serves multiple wayfinding purposes. Good navigation allows merchants to explore topics in depth, switch tasks easily, and filter information.

**Navigation Schemes in Shopify Admin:**
- **Structural:** main navigation, local navigation, breadcrumbs
- **Associative:** contextual links to other features or help documentation
- **Utility:** linked avatars to access your account, search

Example: Merchants can find their way to the blog posts page using the side navigation and search. They can use the breadcrumb to go up to the main level in the Online Store section of the navigation. They can also access related help documentation through contextual "learn more" links.

### 2. Give Content One Home and Many Doors

All people are unique and have different information-seeking behaviors. One person might start their experience from various points in a product or shift their focus midway through a task. They might begin a task on one device and finish it on another. To facilitate these behaviors, all screens should have meaningful navigation and bridge content to other parts of the product.

Example: The Shopify Help Center's shipping page only lives in the Help Center—not duplicated in the Shopify admin or on marketing pages. But even though it only lives in one place, merchants access it when they need more context on shipping (like through a link on the shipping settings page in the Shopify admin).

### 3. Avoid Information Overload

Although we want to give merchants all the information needed to complete a task, we must avoid overloading them with information. Don't over-simplify, but don't burden users with excessive choice. Use progressive disclosure in design and IA:
- Gradually reveal information as it's requested
- Provide multiple access points to information
- Eliminate redundant content

Example: The Shopify Capital page summarizes program benefits at a high level. After scanning the summary, merchants can access more information with a link to detailed docs. By progressively disclosing information, merchants reach decisions faster and avoid frustration and disorientation.

### 4. Plan for Growth and Change

Information architecture, like design, is not set in stone. It should change with your product. The IA decisions you make need to leave room for growth while promoting consistency across the experience.

Example: The Shopify.com homepage takes a scalable, modular approach. Each locale can customize it to meet their needs. For 2 stories, use 2 banners. For 3, use 1 banner and 2 tiles.

**To make sure your IA decisions are scalable:**
- Communicate early and often, within and outside of your team. Open the lines of communication when doing IA work and ensure your work aligns with the work of other areas.
- Think about the future and how your work will intersect with other projects.
- Create content groups flexible enough to accommodate growth while remaining meaningful to users.
- Consider how your IA will adapt to multiple environments and their existing patterns (iOS, Android, desktop browsers, chat bots, retail products like Point of Sale).
